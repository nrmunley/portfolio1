Newlocal's Story

It is often hard to find good places to watch English Premier League soccer matches in the USA. The games are on extremely early in the morning and if you don’t have specific television channels then you can’t view the games. There are bars that open early in order for fans to gather and watch the games, but they are often hard to find. This application will help fans find bars that will be televising the game in cities they are unfamiliar with, very much like a finely tailored version of Yelp.

Research

Initial research was invaluable when it came to beginning the process of defining the projects MVP; learning which location-based applications people use as well as how they use them. Leveraging user surveys and competitive analysis I was able to steer the project in the right direction at an early stage.

Surveys

The goal of the surveys I ran was to understand how people currently use location-based search applications, what the most common location-based search applications are and to find out about location-based search applications that I was unfamiliar with. As well as, the positives and negatives of each. I found that 75% of users tested used Yelp to locate bars and restaurants, but when asked specifically about sports bars there was no common application used. The only trend was that 35% of people surveyed defaulted to Google search when looking for sports bars.

75% of users surveyed use Yelp when doing a general search of restaurants and bars.

35% of users surveyed use Google search when looking for sports bars specifically.

Weaknesses Opportunities
- Old out-dated branding that doesn’t speak to the modern consumer - Can have additional features that competitors don’t offer
- Too much content not targeted in the correct way. - Be more specific with what our users are searching for and target users and business' based on demographics

Competitive Analysis

I did a competitive analysis of three different applications to establish who the potential competition might be and how newlocal could differentiate itself. I looked at the Yelp application, Bar Finder application and EPL Live application and found several weaknesses of these companies leaving ample opportunity to jump into the market.

User Personas

I did several follow up interviews to further define who our users were and what exactly they wanted and needed out of a product like newlocal.

Information Architecture

I used my initial research to help define what was important to have in my MVP and help build the layout of the application.

User Stories

Based on the information gathered I started to develop user stories for a new user and for a returning user. These user stories were then broken down into “MVP", “Nice to Have’s”, and “Unnecessary” to help define what needed to be part of the MVP.

MVP As a returning User, I want to see upcoming match days and match times.
Nice to Have As a returning User, I want to create a group that will get notified if I check-in to watch a game.
Unnecessary As a returning User, I want to chat with other fans within the app.

User Flows

From the user stories, I developed user flows to help visualize the user's interaction with the application and help design how the application would work.

Logo

I developed a narrative to help guide the logo design and began by drawing sketches to help communicate that story. I wanted newlocal to be modern and approachable and focus on the aspect of community and togetherness that comes with being a sports fan. I developed a subtle icon depicting the silhouette of two fans embracing in a cheers.

Wireframes & Mockups

After doing my initial research. Defining my MVP and developing newlocal's brand narrative I started to design the actual layout of the application. Using the user flows I previously generated I began to layout the application through a series of sketching and LoFi and HiFi wireframing which then became my design mockups.

Sketches

I started to layout my ideas using a pen and pad to help visualize what the most important screens of newlocal would look like.

myBalsamiq Wireframes

I used my sketches as a guide and moved those into myBalsamiq to get a better idea of layout sizing. I continued to iterate on the main ‘Map View’ screen because it is the main screen users see and use.

HiFi Wireframes

I then moved the five main screens users would interact with from myBalsamiq into Sketch and started to nail down the exact sizing of elements.

Preference Testing

I still wasn’t that confident in my ‘Map View’ home screen so I did a preference test of three design mockups that I created based off of my HiFi wireframes. 60% of participants tested chose ‘Map View 3’.

Design Mockups

Using my wireframes and my homescreen as a baseline and applying my branding I built out the rest of my design mockup in order to do some user testing.

User Testing

I was able to run user testing on my mockup prototype using InVision to ensure that the navigation within the application was intuitive.

Usability

I tested six different tasks, all of which passed with a 100% completion rate:
(1) Open an article in the ‘Team News’ section (2) Navigate to the ‘League News’ section of the app (3) Edit app notifications (4) Navigate to the ‘Team Stats’ section of the app (5) Edit app preferences (6) Open a search bar in the ‘Team News’ section

Despite all six tasks being completed successfully during user testing, putting the mockups in the hands of users led to some design changes. I moved the search icon to the upper left and the ‘Home’ button to the bottom nav. I also needed to increase the contrast of all the buttons.

HIG Compliance

Scrutiny over whether the search function searched the entire application or just a certain section made me exam not just the design, but brought to light that some sections needed to be redesigned to comply with the HIG.

I solved the search vagueness issue with placeholder text in the search bar as well as a scope bar that followed HIG design standards.

In the ‘Settings’ sections of the application, I made sure to use standard table cell styling and index indicators along the right edge of the table to make the ‘Settings’ sections consistent and comply with the HIG.

What I learned

Sticking to a defined process and moving through focused tasks helped to get the project completed in a timely manner. One of the key things I learned was the importance of documenting my work throughout the process to help show all the design decisions that were made.

This being the first iOS application that I have designed there was definitely a learning curve regarding the use of the HIG. Additionally, I learned that using real content when possible in design mockups helps get better feedback from usability tests because the testable footprint will appear to be 100% functional. I also learned through design critiques from peers, the importance of maintaining consistency throughout the app, even something small like a tweak in opacity can throw the whole design off leading to distrust in the application company and brand.
